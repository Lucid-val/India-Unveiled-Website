Indian
Handicrafts

From time immemorial, India is known for its customs. As far as art and culture are concerned, India features amongst the topmost rated culturally rich countries in the world. The handicrafts of India have been loved and respected worldwide and have left everyone awestruck. Many rural people still earn their livelihood from their creative pieces of art and India has still managed to preserve its artisans, its art and its handicrafts.

Andhra Pradesh

Andhra Pradesh is a hub of diverse and exquisite handicraft traditions. Budithi art, originating in Srikakulam, showcases stunning brassware designs blending traditional and contemporary styles. Mangalagiri sarees from Guntur are known for their elegant Nizam-style weaving and tribal motifs. Etikoppaka toys, crafted with lacquer finish in Visakhapatnam, feature intricate designs and natural dyes. Kondapalli toys, made of painted wood, depict real-life scenes and mythological characters with meticulous craftsmanship. Kalamkari, a hand-painted textile technique, thrives in Srikalahasthi and Machilipatnam, offering vibrant designs on various fabrics. Bidri craft, named after Bidar in Karnataka, showcases silver or gold inlay on black metal, with Mughal-inspired motifs and intricate engraving techniques. These diverse crafts represent the rich cultural heritage and skilled artistry of Andhra Pradesh's artisans.

Arunachal Pradesh

The handicrafts of Arunachal Pradesh are diverse and showcase the rich cultural heritage of the region. Wancho woodworking by the Wancho tribe displays intricate craftsmanship in functional and decorative items. Bamboo and cane products, abundant in the region, are used to create a variety of items like baskets, hats, and jewellery. The art of weaving, predominantly done by women, produces vibrant dress materials and accessories using natural and synthetic dyes. Carpets, particularly made by the Monpas community, are sought after for their attractive designs. Wood carving is another prominent craft, seen in utensils, masks, and religious images, with the Khamptis tribe excelling in this art. Ornamental art includes bamboo jewellery and ornaments made from feathers and beetle wings, showcasing the creativity of Arunachal Pradesh's artisans.

Assam

Assam boasts a rich tradition of handicrafts including pottery by the Kumars and Hiras, bamboo and cane articles used in everyday life, and intricate metal crafts from villages like Hajo and Sarthebari. The state is known for its diverse toys, from clay to bamboo and wooden creations, depicting themes like weddings and animals. Masks play a significant role in Assamese culture, used in dances and made from various materials like wood and terracotta. Additionally, Assam's handloom industry, especially renowned for Muga silk, attracts global recognition with its beautiful and durable fabrics.

Bihar

Bihar's handicrafts are renowned for their cultural richness and artistic depth. Madhubani Paintings from the Mithila region are iconic, depicting vibrant tales and cultural motifs. Manjusha, originating from Bhagalpur, narrates stories like Bihula Vishdhari using limited colors and intricate borders. Tikuli, traditional bindis with gold foil designs, showcases a blend of elegance and tradition. Paper Mache crafts, seen in life-size Matrika figures, display meticulous artistry and devotion. Bamboo crafts, rooted in ancient practices, offer a glimpse into Bihar's historical craftsmanship, from utilitarian items to intricate models. Sikki or Kusha grass crafts, with their golden hues and varied shapes, represent the state's creativity and artisanal skill.

Chattisgarh

Chhattisgarh boasts a rich heritage of handicrafts, including bell metal (Dhokra) crafts known for their intricate designs by tribes like Jharas and Ghadwas. Wrought iron (Loha Shilp) creates raw metal figurines and artifacts from recycled scrap iron. Terracotta pottery reflects tribal life and emotions, while woodcraft (Kashta Shilp) showcases the skills of the Badhais tribe. Godna is a unique art form where women in Jamgala district paint traditional tattoo motifs on textiles using natural colors. Tumba, originating from the Bastar region, involves crafting containers from hollow gourd shells, highlighting the region's innovative crafts.

Goa

Goa's handicrafts are diverse and rich, featuring coir products like hats, mats, masks, and ropes made from coconut husks. Lacquer handicrafts blend metal and wood, including baby carts and toys. Corlim is known for fibre work, creating items like bags and wall hangings from banana or sisal fibre. Jute handicrafts are popular nationwide, with Goan artisans crafting bags, wall hangings, and more. Coconut shells are used for mask faces, and sea shell art creates a range of decorative items like ashtrays and lamp shades. These crafts showcase Goa's creativity and cultural heritage.

Gujarat

Gujarat boasts a diverse range of traditional handicrafts, including lacquer art from Nirona village, silver work from various regions like Anjur and Bhuj, leather handicrafts from Bhujodi village, block printing in Ahmedabad and other areas, Patola sarees from Patan, and Zari embroidery from Surat. Lacquer art involves using natural resin and dyes on wood, while silver work showcases intricate designs in ornaments. Leathercraft combines embroidery with zari work, while block printing uses hand-carved wooden blocks for textile designs. Patola sarees are known for their double ikat weaving and symbolic motifs, while Zari embroidery adds richness to bridal dresses with its elaborate designs. These crafts reflect Gujarat's rich heritage and skilled craftsmanship passed down through generations.

Haryana

Embroidery and weaving are prominent crafts in Haryana, producing shawls, durries, and robes known for their beauty and intricate work. Phulkari, a popular art form, features embroidered motifs of birds, flowers, and human figures on colorful cloth. Durries are rugs with geometric designs, often made in Panipat. Bagh is another craft with colorful geometric patterns, typically done by Muslim artisans. Chope shawls are simpler compared to Phulkari and Bagh. Sculpture, especially of deities, is a traditional craft found in the central and northern regions of Haryana, reflecting the state's rich artistic heritage.

Himachal Pradesh

Chamba Rumal, known for its intricate embroidery, is a cherished gift item from the Chamba kingdom, crafted with silk threads on muslin and malmal fabrics. Himachali topis, adorned with peacock feathers or marigold flowers, symbolize cultural events and are popular in Shimla and Kullu regions. Kullu shawls feature vibrant geometric and floral patterns, available in various wool types like Pashmina and Angora. Pullas, grass shoes from Jalori and Bashleo pass regions, provide warm footwear options. Himachal's metal craft, seen in temple statues and utilitarian items, exhibits exquisite craftsmanship dating back to 600 AD. Tribal jewelry, crafted from silver and gold, showcases unique designs like Kangra's enameling and traditional motifs found in museums across the region.

Jharkhand

Wood craft in Jharkhand utilizes the abundance of wood to create household items like door panels, boxes, and spoons, known for their intricate designs and market appeal. Bamboo from the forests is used for making baskets, hunting, and fishing equipment. Paitkar paintings, an ancient tribal art form, depict life after death but are facing extinction due to lack of support. Tribal ornaments, crafted from gold, silver, or beads, reflect the region's traditional culture. Dhokra, an ancient metal casting art, is prevalent in Jharkhand, featuring motifs inspired by local culture and characterized by a natural style. Terracotta, traditionally used in temple architecture, is crafted into jars, vases, and figurines by the Kumhar community.

Karnataka

Mysore paintings, characterized by elegance and intricate detail, are a significant part of South Indian art, akin to Tanjore paintings. Ivory carving, prominent in Karnataka, showcases gods, goddesses, and intricate designs. Sandalwood carving in Karnataka is renowned for its detailed figurines and natural motifs, using the region's abundant sandalwood resources. Kasuti embroidery, a traditional craft in Karnataka, features feminine designs on sarees and blouses. Chittara folk art, practiced by the Deewaru community, depicts auspicious celebrations with natural materials like ground rice paste. Bidriware, originating from Persia and brought to India by the Bahamani Sultans, showcases intricate designs on various items like bowls, trays, and jewellery. These crafts reflect Karnataka's rich artistic heritage and cultural diversity.

Kerala

Kerala showcases a diverse range of handicrafts, including ivory products known for delicate crafting, especially in mythological figures. Coir products like mattresses and decorated mats are famous, with Kollam and Calicut being key cities for coir and cane crafts. Wooden handicrafts in Kerala draw inspiration from Hindu temples, producing decorative pieces like table lamps and toys. Sandalwood products such as decorated boxes and Kathakali dance postures are also renowned. Screw Pine Weaving creates mats, hats, and bags from pine leaves. Banana handicrafts are rising in popularity, crafting items like table mats and sarees. Kathakali dance masks, made from eco-friendly materials, symbolize Kerala's rich cultural heritage.

Madhya Pradesh

Bagh Prints from Madhya Pradesh showcase dynamic floral and geometrical prints using wooden blocks and vegetable dyes, reflecting cultural significance. Zari Zardozi embroidery from Bhopal, dating back to Rig Vedic times, embellishes garments with metallic threads, stones, and sequins, evoking a royal aura. Batto Bai Dolls from Jhabua depict tribal features, serving as souvenirs and educational tools. Gond Painting from Patangarh embodies colorful tribal life, narrating stories on various mediums. Nandana Prints from Tarapur showcase mud resist block printing with traditional motifs influenced by nomadic life. Leather Animals from Indore are meticulously crafted by craftsmen and families, catering to taxidermy enthusiasts with lifelike precision. These crafts not only reflect artistic mastery but also cultural narratives and sustainable practices, preserving heritage and storytelling traditions.

Maharashtra

Paithani Saree from Maharashtra is renowned for its handwoven silk and metal thread designs, symbolizing opulence and cultural heritage, recognized as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. Warli Art, originating from Warli district, Maharashtra, reflects tribal life and nature, evolving into contemporary paintings and textiles. Kolhapuri Chappal, a notable Maharashtra craft, showcases premium leather craftsmanship with traditional and modern designs. Nath, a traditional Maharashtrian nosepin, signifies marital status and beauty with gold and diamonds. Narayan Peth Saree, a classic Maharashtrian silk saree, features intricate motifs and vibrant colors. Mashru combines cotton and silk for elegance and comfort, while Himroo blends metallic and silk threads for opulent textiles, both rooted in historical weaving styles. These crafts embody Maharashtra's rich artistic heritage, blending tradition with contemporary trends.

Manipur

Manipur's famous handicrafts encompass a rich tapestry of artistic traditions, including hand-woven textiles adorned with intricate embroidery, durable mats crafted from water reed, charming Manipuri dance dolls capturing the essence of classical dance forms, warm shawls and blankets featuring intricate designs, vibrant Manipuri bed covers of Moirangfee showcasing traditional motifs, skillfully crafted cane and bamboo work ranging from furniture to decorative items, and exquisite wood carvings adorning various objects from furniture to religious artifacts. These handicrafts not only reflect Manipur's cultural heritage but also highlight the craftsmanship and artistic prowess of its skilled artisans.

Meghalaya

Meghalaya's rich tradition of handicrafts showcases skilled craftsmanship in weaving, especially in cane and cloth. The state's markets offer a treasure trove of handmade items, with woodcarving and cane/bamboo work standing out due to abundant wood resources. Khasis excel in weaving durable cane mats ('Tlieng') and crafting domestic tools like knives and utensils, while Garos specialize in 'Dakmanda' fabric for clothing and produce beautiful baskets like 'Meghum Khoks.' Garo artisans also master Pokerwork on bamboo and make intricate fishing traps. Jaintia tribes' expertise lies in bamboo traps and other crafts. Besides, Meghalaya is known for silk and carpet weaving, ornaments, and musical instruments. Shillong's bazaars like Police Bazar and Khadi Gramodyog offer a wide range of these handicrafts, known for their appeal and durability, making them prized souvenirs for visitors.

Mizoram

Cane and bamboo handicrafts are a significant craft in Mizoram, known for their simplicity, durability, and wide range of products like baskets, utensils, hats, and furniture. These crafts have a strong market both locally and beyond, with government emporia offering them at reasonable prices. Basketry, a popular craft among Mizoram's tribes, showcases intricate designs and versatile usage. Weaving is another skilled craft, producing beautiful fabrics with traditional patterns, notably the Puandum handloom fabric, essential in Mizo culture, especially during marriages and traditional dances like Khuallam. Mizo Shawls, made from light wool, are handmade treasures from the hills of Northeast India, suitable for all seasons and embodying the region's rich weaving traditions.

Nagaland

Nagaland's rich cultural tapestry is intricately woven with art and handicrafts, reflecting the craftsmanship of its 16 tribes. Utilizing resources from farms, fields, and forests, they create a range of items, from clothes to ritual objects, showcasing self-sufficiency. Naga villages boast impressive gates, house posts, and Morungs, testifying to the state's artisanal heritage. Handicrafts from Nagaland, renowned for their vibrant patterns and quality, are in high demand nationally and internationally, especially the famous Nagaland shawls with tribe-specific designs. Cane and bamboo work, wood carving, blacksmithy, and pottery are prominent crafts, with the Konyak tribe excelling in wood carving. Intricate figures like human forms, animals, and symbols adorn their wooden creations. Abundant bamboo resources fuel the creation of baskets, hats, jewelry, and decorative items, showcasing Naga ingenuity. Despite taboos in some tribes, blacksmithy thrives, particularly among the skilled Konyaks, highlighting Nagaland's diverse and enduring craft traditions.

Odisha

The crafts of Odisha, including pottery, horn works, tribal jewelry, traditional art forms like Jhoti chita and Pattachitra, puppetry, paintings, stone and wood carving, and silver filigree, showcase a rich cultural heritage. These crafts, passed down through generations, reflect intricate designs, vibrant colors, and a blend of traditional and modern elements. From terracotta artisans producing decorative items to skilled woodcarvers crafting intricate figurines, Odisha's crafts are a testament to artistic excellence and cultural diversity. Silver filigree from Cuttack and tribal jewelry made using ancient techniques add to the uniqueness of Odisha's craft traditions, making them highly sought-after and cherished pieces of art and utility.

Punjab

Punjab's traditional arts and crafts are deeply rooted in its culture, showcasing the skills of its artisans across various mediums. Mud work, a centuries-old practice, transforms walls into artistic expressions, often performed by rural women. Weaving and embroidery, like Phulkari and Bagh, adorn fabrics with vibrant motifs and are integral to Punjabi life. Woodworks display intricate carvings and furniture, while basketry and folk toys highlight local craftsmanship. Doll-making is a flourishing industry, especially in Chandigarh. Peedhies, traditional stools, blend functionality with artistic flair. These crafts not only reflect Punjab's heritage but also have a global appeal, contributing significantly to the state's handicraft industry.

Rajasthan

Rajasthan's artisanal heritage is diverse and rich, with a focus on materials like stone, leather, ivory, and textiles. Stonecraft shines with sandstone, limestone, and marble, seen in palaces and havelis. Pottery varies across regions, from Jaipur's blue pottery to Bikaner's lac-decorated pieces. Camel leather is used extensively for shoes, bags, and decor, reflecting Rajasthan's desert culture. Ivory bangles and Rajasthani Razai silk quilts are prized traditional items. Dhurries, flat rugs from Rajasthan, and Lac Bangles, adorned with pearls and stones, are symbolic of Rajasthan's vibrant artistry and cultural traditions, cherished as souvenirs and symbols of the region's craftsmanship.

Sikkim

Sikkim, nestled in northeastern India, showcases a vibrant culture steeped in artistic traditions. Thangkas, intricate Tibetan Buddhist paintings, hold spiritual significance and are crafted with meticulous detail using natural materials. The state's mastery in carving is evident in the Namthang style, known for floral designs adorning wooden panels and Buddhist symbols. Sikkimese women excel in carpet weaving, producing durable woolen carpets with traditional motifs. Handloom textiles, including thara, reflect generations of weaving expertise, creating shawls, jackets, and other garments. Bamboo and cane crafts are popular for their eco-friendly nature, crafting furniture, baskets, and decorative items. Metalwork in brass and copper yields exquisite lamps, utensils, and Buddhist figurines, each intricately designed and imbued with cultural significance.

Tamilnadu

Tamil Nadu's rich cultural heritage reflects in its handicrafts, influenced by dynasties like Pallavas and Cholas. Rock-cut temples and Dravidian architecture showcase this legacy. Music instruments like Naadaswaram and Kumbu are tied to cultural occasions. Traditional Tamil jewelry uses stones for protection, seen in ornaments like Oddiyaanam and Jimiki. Tanjore paintings, woodwork, and ivory art highlight artistic prowess. Silk weaving, especially the Kanjeevaram Sari, is a significant profession, cherished by locals and visitors alike for its exquisite craftsmanship. Tamil Nadu's handicrafts blend tradition with skill, making them prized cultural artifacts.

Telengana

Telangana's handicrafts are diverse and steeped in tradition. Pochampally Ikat Weaving from Bhoodan Pochampally village showcases resist-dyed threads creating vibrant sarees with intricate geometric patterns. Cheriyal Scroll Painting from Cheriyal village features mythological figures in vivid colors and elaborate motifs. Nirmal Toys and Crafts, originating from Nirmal district, offer handmade wooden toys painted in bright hues. Bidriware, hailing from Bidar city, showcases intricate metal designs using a blackened zinc-copper alloy. These crafts reflect Telangana's rich cultural heritage and skilled craftsmanship, attracting art enthusiasts and collectors worldwide.

Tripura

Tripura's handicrafts showcase the state's rich cultural heritage and skilled artisans. Bamboo and cane work are prominent, with products like room dividers, wall panels, silver jewelry, lamps, and furniture. Handloom weaving, deeply ingrained in tribal traditions, features vibrant fabrics with intricate designs and embroidery. Woodcraft utilizes the state's dense forests for trays, statues, and utensils with detailed craftsmanship. Cane and bamboo craft, a major industry, produces diverse items like handbags, fans, and table mats, renowned for their durability and appeal. Basket weaving is another art, creating sturdy yet elegant baskets for various household uses. Furniture like the 'mudha' stool exemplifies Tripura's blend of traditional materials and skilled craftsmanship, making these handicrafts both beautiful and functional.

Uttar Pradesh

The crafts of Uttar Pradesh showcase a rich heritage of artistry. Chikankari embroidery from Lucknow is famed for its delicate patterns on white fabric, perfect for summer attire. Varanasi Brocade, known as Kinkab, dazzles with silk fabrics adorned in golden and silver threads, a favorite of Indian brides. Agra's stonecraft produces exquisite marble artifacts, while carpets from Bhadohi, Shahjahanpur, and Mirzapur feature Taj Mahal motifs. Wooden toys from Varanasi display intricate designs of animals and flowers. Sanjhi Art, a unique form, involves cutting geometric designs into paper, often depicting Lord Krishna and Radha.

Uttarakhand

Uttarakhand's handicrafts reflect its rich cultural heritage and artistic prowess. Aipan, a ritualistic folk art, adorns auspicious occasions and daily objects with intricate designs. Ringaal handicrafts, crafted from bamboo, include baskets and furniture from the Kumaon region. Handloom handicrafts feature jute and hemp creations like bags and wall hangings. The state is known for its Angora and Pashmina woolens, especially from Almora. Kumaoni pottery showcases detailed designs on functional and decorative items. Garhwali jewelry is adorned with semi-precious stones and intricate patterns. Pithoragarh paintings depict local legends, while Nainital woodwork displays intricate carvings on furniture and decorative pieces, highlighting Uttarakhand's artistic diversity and craftsmanship.

West Bengal

Sholapith, a natural material from Bengal and Assam, surpasses artificial thermocole in malleability and beauty, ideal for intricate ornamentation. Bengal's jute industry thrives with products like carpets, handbags, and bedspreads. Clay dolls from Krishnagar, patronized by Maharaja Krishnachandra, are a significant art form. Bamboo and cane artisans in West Bengal create a wide range of everyday and decorative items. Kantha embroidery, originating from rural Bengal, showcases centuries-old stitching traditions. Muslin from regions like Dhaka and Murshidabad was renowned for its quality. Brass and bell metal crafts from Bankura, Bishnupur, and Ghatal are cherished for their engraved designs and utility.
